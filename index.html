Ocean Characteristics

All oceans are sewawater. All seawater shares roughly the same salinity, which is a measure of solids dissolved in water. On average, every kilogram of seawater contains about 35 grams of salt. Oceans are split into three zones based off of temperature, salinity, and density.

The Five Oceans

Pacific Ocean

Atlantic Ocean

The second largest ocean, covering around 20% of Earth's surface and spanning more than 41 million square miles. It separates the Americas from Africa, Asia, and Europe.

Indian Ocean

The third largest and warmest ocean in the world. Located between India, Africa, and the Indonesian Islands, it is nearly six times the size of the United States.

Southern Ocean

Surrounds Antarctica and is known for intense storms and very cold temperatures. It is mostly covered in ice for the majority of the year.

Arctic Ocean

The world's smallest and shallowest ocean. Located near the North Pole, it is covered by ice for part of the year.

Ocean Zones

Zone Factors

Oceans are separated into layers based on three key characteristics: Salinity, Temperature, and Density.

  • Salinity: In warmer oceans, the top 500 meters are saltier because water evaporates faster, leaving salt behind.
  • Temperature: Temperatures change from the surface down to 1000 meters, varies depending on the ocean.
  • Density: Cold water is denser than warm water, and saltwater is denser than freshwater, causing the water to layer.

Surface Zone

Extends from the surface down to approximately 200 meters. This zone has plenty of sunlight and heat, making it the home for organisms that perform photosynthesis and animals like whales, sharks, and jellyfish.

Twilight Zone

Extends from 200 meters to approximately 1000 meters, where sunlight can no longer reach. Creatures swim up to the surface zone to hunt and feed.

Deep Zone

The largest ecosystem on Earth, existing in complete darkness from 1000 to 4000 meters. Organisms here produce their own light through a chemical process called bioluminescence.
